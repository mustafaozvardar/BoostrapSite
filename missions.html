About Space Missions

A spacecraft or spaceship is a vehicle or machine designed to operate outside the Earth's atmosphere, especially in outer space. Spacecraft may be manned or unmanned. A spacecraft may be designed for telecommunications, Earth observation, meteorology, navigation, space colonisation, planetary exploration, space tourism, space warfare, and the transport of people and cargo in space. This definition also includes artificial satellites. During sub-orbital spaceflight, a spacecraft enters space and then returns to the surface without entering a stable orbit. During orbital spaceflight, the spacecraft enters a closed orbit around the Earth or other astronomical bodies. Spacecraft developed for manned spaceflight are used to carry crew or passengers from launch on Earth or from orbiting space stations. Robotic spacecraft, on the other hand, operate either autonomously (autonomous robot) or under remote control. Robotic spacecraft that support scientific research are called space probes. Robotic spacecraft orbiting a planetary body are called artificial satellites. Only a few interstellar probes, such as Pioneer 10 and 11, Voyager 1 and 2 and New Horizons, are on a trajectory to leave the Solar System. Some orbital spacecraft are designed to be recoverable. Spacecraft that have begun to come back towards the Earth by re-entry can be categorised into two classes: wingless space capsules and winged space planes. Although humanity has achieved spaceflight capability, only a few nations have the necessary technology for orbital launches: Russia (RSA), USA (NASA), European Space Agency (ESA) member states, Japan (JAXA), China (CNSA), India (ISRO), Taiwan[1][2][3][4] (Chung-Shan National Institute of Science and Technology - ing: NCIST-, Taiwan National Space Organisation- ing: NSPO-[5][6][7]), Israel (ISA), Iran (ISA) and North Korea (National Aerospace Development Administration - ing: NADA).
