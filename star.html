About the Stars

A star is a sphere of plasma, composed mainly of hydrogen and helium, that shines in dark space and appears as a dot in the sky. Galaxies formed by clustered stars dominate the observable universe. There are approximately 6 thousand stars that can be seen with the naked eye from Earth. The closest star to the Earth is the Sun, which is also the source of the heat and light necessary for life on Earth. The Sun is the source of most of the energy on Earth, including sunlight. Other stars are visible in the sky at night, when they are not in the light of the Sun when viewed from the Earth. The reason why stars shine is that the nuclear energy released in the nuclear fusion reactions occurring in their cores spreads to outer space by radiation after passing through the star. By observing a star's spectrum, brightness, and motion in space, astronomers can determine its mass, age, chemical composition, and many other properties. The total mass of a star is the main determinant of the star's development and demise. The lifespan of a star can be estimated by looking at its mass: Large stars live less, while small stars like the Sun live longer. The development process of a star is determined by its diameter, rotation, motion and temperature depending on the stage it is in. The Hertzsprung-Russell diagram (H-R diagram), where stars are plotted according to their temperature and brightness, is used to determine the current age and stage of development of stars. The first cycle of star development; It is a cloud of matter consisting of hydrogen, some helium, and very small amounts of heavier elements that begins to collapse inward. After the star core becomes dense enough, some of the hydrogen in it is continuously converted into helium by the nuclear fusion reaction. The rest of the star carries the released energy away from the core by a combination of radiation and convection. These processes prevent the star from collapsing in on itself, and the energy radiates out into outer space, creating a stellar wind on the star's surface.
