About the Universe

The Universe is the entirety and content of space and time, including planets, stars, galaxies and all other matter and energy structures. However, the observable universe is limited to the current arrangement of all matter and energy, starting from fundamental particles to large-scale structures such as galaxies and galaxy clusters. Since there cannot be 'existence' and 'anti-existence' when energy waves or particles are dissolved homogeneously and balancedly, or an explosion cannot occur if their total opposites are in 'annihilation', is the universe cooling down or warming up? Is the cessation of the universe the end? Is the Big Bang the center of the universe? Problems such as "Is it the beginning of the universe?" or "Is the sun at the center of the universe?" have become popular questions about motion or, in other words, time. The most widely accepted theory about the formation of the universe today is the Big Bang theory. According to this theory, the universe was formed by the explosion of a compressed point with zero volume and a very high energy potential. Even today, no fully scientific answer has been found to the questions of how the first explosion occurred, what was in the universe before the universe came into being, or what the universe expanded into, however, hypotheses have been put forward about the pre-universe state and extra-universe existence. They acted independently of each other for a long period as a result of the Big Bang. Independent gases combined to form galaxies (galaxies) by means of the law of gravity, one of the laws of physics that applies everywhere in the ever-expanding universe. As a result of the same universal law of physics, galaxies also approached each other and formed huge groups. Stars and systems formed around some stars within galaxies. The Solar System we live in is one of them. It is estimated that there are more than 400 billion galaxies and 300 sextillion (3 × 1023) stars in the universe that we can explore.
